2. The LEGO Story

So let me tell you how I started to think about thinking about programming:

Long time ago I worked at LEGO. You know LEGO - small plastic bricks you step on painfully when someone was playing with them last night eating pizza and watching The Breakfast Club. At that time, computers and programming was somewhat more exotic than today, so I often heard the explanation that programming was just like playing with LEGO. And it just pissed med off. Good ole fashion off pissed mood, whenever that happened.

I mean, programming is just not like LEGO. Programming is more like sculpturing using clay. You have to create every little detail yourself, and it takes some degree of mastery to get something that actually looks like something (No github, npm or stackoverflow in those days). LEGO is like getting quickly to an approximation of what you want to build. What makes it fun is that you often change your mind during the build, that you start build the thing that it ends up looking like.

Then one day I started thinking about how a programming language would look like if it actually were just like LEGO. To have the properties of LEGO. That getting a new box would allow you to just snap the pieces together with the ones from your old boxes. That you can build parts of a model and snap the parts together as if they were pieces themselves.

That was the start of a 20 year+ quest. Years and years of looking at new computer languages, thinking 'Nah, not like that'. Snippets of eureka! moments follow by long months of draught. Well, I was doing whatever job I was holding at the time, so it's not like I was idle. But a lot of my ideas were pretty radical, in the neighborhood of writing a operating system from scratch, so I still hoped that someone out there, saw some of the ideas, picked up the gauntlet and created that simple, elegant language that is Dawn.

I waited and waited. The I waited some more. It's too radically different for someone to think it a good idea, I thought. I' not even sure it's a good idea. It just exists in my mind, for no one to see.

And yet, there I was. One little man at a stupid little desk (and my hair was starting to grey) out of billions of people living on a floating blue rock. How could I make Dawn happen? Maybe just for a userbase of one, just for myself, because that is how the best things are made - people making good stuff for themselves, that others can appreciate.

The gravestone:

He thought about Dawn, when Evening arrived.

Not at bad epitaph, but meaningless since no one would know what the hell Dawn was.

3. The Dawn of Dawn

So, now you’re wondering how I ever got around to implementing Dawn. The quick answer is: the time was right.

Like when you meet that girl in the yellow dress and you want to kiss her, but you know that she just got dumped by her boyfriend and if you try to take advantage it will end up in tears, Teddy!

Six months later, somehow, you are holding her hand next to the Ferris wheel and the moment it reaches the top you finally know the time is right and you kiss her and it's better than cotton candy and popcorn.

To elaborate a little about how the time was right, there was another quick answer: javascript. The moment it became possible to use javascript on the server as well as the browser, there was a platform to piggyback my way to an implementation.

Just as why I should offer you the best reasons to spend a little time with Dawn and finish the tutorial.

  1. Abstraction.

    Programming is often about getting into nitty gritty itchy bitchy details of programming. Dawn will allow you to do this low level style of programming. But mostly you can stay on a higher level using the sensible default behavior.

    Expanding the library. Dawn really allows you to expand the library with new functionality, that hooks into the existing framework, so you really want to expand the functionality of the library.

  2. One man on one peninsula.

    Dawn was born on the Jütland peninsula, Denmark. Denmark is sort of known for its programming languages. Why so many Danes takes it upon themselves to create programming languages is a mystery.

    And so, here we have Dawn. Created out of the idea that programming should be as easy as building with LEGO bricks.

  3. Free and customizable.

    Using Dawn costs nothing. The code to Dawn itself is open for all of the world to inhale/exhale. Well, it's also not entirely done, so you can actually influence it.

    Dawn is e.g. not about strong types in the current iteration, but you can actually add them on a later date, if that is your hobby horse. Because of how it is strung together it's highly customizable.

    So, it's free and you can freely mold it into something of your liking.

With that, it’s time for the tutorial to begin. You can bring out your notepad, if you don't want to take screenshots or record the entire session. Go on, the tutorial will be waiting right here for you when you get back.
